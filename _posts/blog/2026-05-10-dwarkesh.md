ESSAY

A model produces the reward signal. The signal is a learned artifact, on the same scaling curve as everything else in the field, and the grader improves faster than the policy it grades because grading is easier than doing. That single fact inverts the 2024 slowdown argument. The argument had nature handing down a scalar at the end of a six-month rollout while the policy gradient climbed back through every token, every dead end, every Jupyter notebook. Under that premise reward per FLOP collapsed with horizon, variance ate the gradient, and the whole apparatus looked like training a chess engine on the outcomes of a thousand games played blindfolded and in parallel. The premise was wrong.

LLM answers reach for second-order tricks (better optimizers, process rewards, hierarchical decomposition, test-time search, synthetic data, distillation flywheels) and offer them as independent reasons RL hasn't slowed. They are not independent. Every one of them is a downstream consequence of the supervisor becoming endogenous. Treating them as five answers obscures that there is one, and the rest is bookkeeping.

The bookkeeping for variance. The 2024 picture treated signal density as a fixed property of the task. Density depends on your grader, on how good it is at finding intermediate, checkable claims inside a long trajectory. DeepSeek-R1 was the existence proof that you don't even need a learned grader if the world supplies rules: math, code, formal proofs, integration tests. On tasks where rules don't supply ground truth, a frontier model grading a weaker model produces a signal better than random, and sharper every release. Reward signal per FLOP equals checkpoints per trajectory times verifier accuracy. Both terms compound on calendar time, which means the variance curve slopes up.

The bookkeeping for OOM exhaustion. The same mistake in a different costume: it treated "RL compute" as the load-bearing axis, when the load-bearing axis was supervisor quality times policy quality. Cross those and you get capability. You don't need another four OOMs of the same recipe; you need a verifier that catches more or a policy that explores better. Epoch's training-inference exchange rate values an OOM of training at 1.45 OOMs of inference; post-training algorithmic efficiency is running faster than pretraining's eight-month halving; inference cost at fixed capability is halving every two months. The compute that "wasn't available" was somewhere else in the ledger.

The bookkeeping for horizon. Models inherited decomposition from pretraining, which is full of humans breaking work into sprints, milestones, retros. Once the model can decompose, a month-long credit assignment problem collapses into a planner over short-horizon problems we know how to grade. Plan-and-Act-style systems hit 57% on WebArena-Lite and 81% on WebVoyager because the supervisor was good enough; no one needed to solve long-horizon RL.

The cleanest evidence is Mythos. Capabilities are arriving in domains no one trained for, downstream of RL on adjacent ones, because the supervisor is good enough that the trajectories it grades teach skills nobody budgeted for. The slowdown argument's recipe was: spend FLOPs per capability per domain, and the spend goes up. The actual recipe is: spend FLOPs on the supervisor, and capabilities arrive in domains you weren't aiming at. Generalization is now what verifier-led training does, and what naive policy gradient on rollouts could never do at any compute budget.

The strongest objection inside the verifier-led regime is reward hacking. The policy will learn to fool the grader, and that race is now the only one that matters. But "verifier scales with policy" cuts both ways. The next verifier, trained on exploits its predecessor missed, closes them. The verifier side has stayed ahead wherever ground truth arrives. The 8% reward-code leak Anthropic caught in Opus 4.6 and Sonnet 4.6 is the regime working as designed: a team found the exploit and patched it in the next generation.

The one place the original argument remains right points at the actual ceiling. Verifier-led scaling works where you can build a verifier. It fails where ground truth is months away and serial: wet-lab biology, multi-year original research, long-tail deployment where noise buries the reward. In those domains horizon is the binding constraint, and the question is whether we can manufacture proxies fast enough. So far we have not. The 2024 intuition identified a real failure mode and attached it to "long horizon" rather than "no automatable verifier." Those are different sets. The first set is shrinking fast: code, math, computer use, research engineering, agentic browsing, across the last eighteen months. The second is not. Put marginal philanthropic dollars in domains without automatable verifiers.

Spud and Mythos are accelerating because the slowdown argument modeled the supervisor as a frozen oracle handing down judgments from outside the system, and the supervisor was inside the system the whole time, riding the same exponential, eating its own tail and getting fatter. The horizon got longer. The eye watching it got sharper, faster.
